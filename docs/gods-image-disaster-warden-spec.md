# God’s Image: Disaster Warden — Art Style Spec Sheet

**Project:** Horror.Place  
**Style ID:** GI-Disaster-Warden-001  
**Classification:** God-Scale Environmental Horror Style  
**Intended Use:** Horror.Place only (game-worlds, AI-generated movies, concept art)

---

## 1. Concept Overview

“God’s Image: Disaster Warden” is a horror art-style where god-like entities are expressed as environmental forces and reflective scars across a ruined world, not as ordinary characters. The style replaces pen-and-ink with a chrome-brush technique and hand-painted silhouettes, using reflection and silhouette to encode divine judgment, environmental disasters, and territorial beasts that keep humans out of forbidden land.

The core thesis: the landscape itself is the face and body of a hostile, regulating god. Skies, ground, water, and buildings become warning sigils, scars, and wardens, rendered with vivid reflective detail and minimal literal gore or explicit violence.

---

## 2. Visual Language

### 2.1 Core Aesthetic

- **Primary Look:** High-contrast silhouettes carved out by chrome-like reflections, set against matte, storm-dark bases.
- **Key Motif:** “Divine exclusion zones” — areas where reflection, light, and scale clearly warn humans away.
- **God-Body Environments:** Cliffs, clouds, reservoirs, and city blocks form implied anatomy (faces, ribs, halos) at macro scale.
- **Beasts as Wardens:** Territorial, god-made beasts defined mainly by reflective rim-light and segmented silhouette, not by detailed anatomy.

### 2.2 Chrome-Brush Technique

- No pen & ink; forms are shaped by **specular strokes** and highlight bands instead of line hatching.
- Brushes simulate brushed chrome, liquid metal, and polished bone: narrow, directional strokes with strong value jumps.
- Reflections remember a dominant “divine light vector” (e.g., a halo, rift, or reactor), bending around silhouettes and structures.
- Base materials (soil, cloud, concrete) stay matte and desaturated; **only edges and select surfaces receive chrome**.

### 2.3 Hand-Painted Silhouettes

- Characters, gods, and beasts are rendered as dense silhouettes with minimal internal detail.
- Volume is implied via rim-light, layered reflection bands, and subtle value breaks, never via full anatomical rendering.
- Faces appear as emergent patterns in clouds, water, and layered silhouettes, using 3–7 key shapes (eyes, mouth, brow arcs) to suggest expression without explicit portraiture.

---

## 3. Color & Lighting

### 3.1 Palette Principles

- **Base Family:** Void black, bruised purple, charcoal gray.
- **Structural Family:** Concrete gray, oxidized metal, muted rust.
- **Divine Accent:** Razor-bright chrome highlights that pick up limited hazard colors:
  - Toxic teal/green (environmental poisoning)
  - Furnace orange (industrial/cataclysmic fire)
  - Emergency cyan/white (artificial divine light, siren tone)

Rule: Intense color lives **inside reflections and glows**, not in base materials. The world is subtracted; its warnings are oversaturated.

### 3.2 Lighting Logic

- Multiple competing light sources:
  - Primary “divine” source (halo, rift, star-reactor).
  - Secondary local sources (industrial fires, warning beacons).
- Chrome reflections prioritize the divine source and then fold in local hazards, creating layered highlight hierarchies.
- Shadows are deep and continuous; silhouettes often merge into background, separated primarily by rim-light and reflection streaks.

---

## 4. Environmental Design

### 4.1 Sky as Omen

- Cloudbanks host chrome-vein structures: branching, reflective tendrils that resemble nerves, rivers, or fractured glass.
- Large-scale faces and sigils can be implied by arranging highlight clusters (eyes, brow, mouth) across clouds or auroras.
- Sky tones: dark blues, purples, and near-black, with hazard colors confined to reflective veins and halos.

### 4.2 Ground and Water

- Ground is cracked, flooded, and partially glassed; puddles and basins behave as low-resolution mirrors.
- Reflective liquids (oil, ash-water, mercury-like pools) show warped god-faces and beast forms at steep angles.
- Where beasts patrol, reflections become more distorted and noisy (visual indicator of territorial threat).

### 4.3 Architecture as Seal and Scar

- Buildings blend Machine-Canyon’s industrial ruin with divine overpainting:
  - Brutalist slabs, pipes, ducts, tanks.
  - Edges and joints picked out in chrome, often bent or warped into claw- or fang-like contours.
- Large structures can form:
  - **Seals:** circular pits rimmed in shining metal, closed doors to forbidden cores.
  - **Crowns/Halos:** radial arrays of shining panels or spires around a void.
  - **Wounds:** collapsed towers whose exposed interiors act as bright, reflective crosses or ribs.

---

## 5. Character and Beast Representation

### 5.1 God-Faces

- Rarely drawn explicitly; appear as:
  - Cloud formations where chrome arcs align into an eye and jaw.
  - Cliff faces where reflective streaks suggest a mask.
  - Water surfaces where reflection break-up implies watchful eyes.
- No clear pupils or human detail; faces are archetypal, mask-like, and slightly unstable.

### 5.2 Disaster Wardens (Beasts)

- Purpose: keep humans away from regions marked as environmentally or spiritually forbidden.
- Silhouettes: elongated, segmented, often quadrupedal or many-limbed, with exaggerated spines and tails implied by repeating reflective segments.
- No gore: threat is communicated via mass, posture, silence, and reflection patterns, not open wounds or viscera.
- Motion: slow, deliberate, like patrol automata or living statues powered by light.

### 5.3 Human Figures

- Small, vulnerable silhouettes, often clustered or solitary along reflective axes (bridges, shorelines, catwalks).
- Rim-lit by the same chrome that defines gods and beasts, tying them into the divine light network and reinforcing their exposure.

---

## 6. Composition & Camera

### 6.1 Layout Rules

- Use strong vertical or diagonal “divine axes” of reflection (sky → architecture → water) to connect god, beast, and human.
- Place human silhouettes at intersections of these axes to amplify scale contrast.
- Reserve upper-thirds for sky-omens and colossal faces; mid-grounds for industrial and geological mass; lower-thirds for water, rubble, and figures.

### 6.2 Motion and Animation Cues

- In moving scenes: allow subtle drift of reflections across static silhouettes to give a sense of divine surveillance.
- Environmental changes (storms, floods, power surges) should increase chrome intensity and hazard-color saturation, signaling escalation.

---

## 7. Thematic Constraints & Safety

- No explicit gore or close-up physical harm; horror comes from scale, inevitability, environmental collapse, and divine indifference.
- Violence is implied through:
  - Collapsed infrastructure
  - Abandoned settlements
  - Hazard reflections and exclusion zones
- Stylistic goal: transform destructive or apocalyptic intent into **abstract, reflective, god-scale warnings** rather than literal scenes of harm.
