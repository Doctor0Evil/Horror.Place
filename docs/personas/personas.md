## Persona kinds and contracts

Personas are registered as entries in the `personas` registry, each conforming to `registry-personas.v1.json` and the base registry schema, with IDs like `PER-ARAL-0001` and opaque `artifactid`/`deadledgerref` wiring them to implementation assets in the constellation. Each persona contract formalizes four axes: type semantics, historical anchoring, behavior profile, and invariant coupling, which together tell engines and agents how this persona is allowed to exist and act. The **personaType** axis splits into `npc`, `spectral-entity`, `environmental-agent`, and `narrative-voice`, giving you a spectrum from grounded conversational characters to disembodied narrators or system-level environmental behaviors. An `npc` persona contract will emphasize dialogue, quest hooks, and transactional behaviors, while a `spectral-entity` contract leans into stalking, manifestation rules, and spectral plausibility constraints via SPI and SHCI. `environmental-agent` personas push agency into things like architecture, wind, or anomalous weather, binding their behavior to CIC/AOS and other invariants rather than a humanoid actor. `narrative-voice` personas govern how information is delivered—radio voices, diaries, internal monologues—designed to manipulate EMD and CDL while remaining anchored to specific historical events. ## NDJSON persona registry structure

All of this is surfaced through NDJSON registries that replace ad-hoc discovery, so *persona contracts are found and enforced* via registry lines, not by walking directories. At the NDJSON level, every registry entry—regions, events, styles, personas—shares the same core fields: `id`, `schemaref`, `deadledgerref`, `artifactid`, `createdAt`, and `status` (`active`, `deprecated`, `archived`, `draft`), plus optional `tags`, `tier`, `invariantBindings`, and `metricTargets`. IDs follow a strict `<TYPE>-<REGION-CODE>-<SEQUENCE>` pattern, so persona entries look like `PER-ARAL-0001` and are immutable once published, with deprecation handled via new entries pointing to replacements. Personas live in a `personas.ndjson` (production) or `personas.example.ndjson` (examples) file, each line being a compact JSON object with no comments or pretty-printing, encoded as UTF‑8 with LF endings and a final newline. A persona line includes all base registry fields and then persona-specific fields: `personaType`, `historicalAnchor`, `behaviorProfile`, `invariantCoupling`, and optional `spectralConsistencyIndex`, `metricInfluence`, `prismMetaRef`, and `experimentalFlags`. ## Correspondence to events, regions, and styles

Personas are not isolated; they sit in a triangle with events and regions, and styles wrap the whole thing, all mediated through their NDJSON registries and the spine index. **Region** entries (`REG-*`) define spatial or conceptual tiles with `invariantProfile`, `biomeTags`, and history, plus optional fields like LSG, SPI, RRM, and FCF; events and personas reference these by IDs, and tools can compute “which personas are valid in this region” by intersecting invariants and tiers. **Event** entries (`EVT-*`) encode `eventType`, `regionIds`, `triggerConditions`, and `invariantRequirements`, optionally listing `personaIds` to declare which personas can participate, and mapping telemetry hooks (UEC, EMD, STCI, etc.) so drama managers can see their effects. **Style** entries (`STY-*`) declare `styleCategory`, `targetTier`, `aestheticDescriptors`, and `invariantAlignment`, plus optional `metricTargets` and `engineHints`, and regions/events/personas can reference them via `styleIds` to define aesthetic envelopes in which these agents act.
